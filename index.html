A Summer of National Parks Part II

February 5, 2025 • 8 min read

The stunning Olympic Mountains in Washington State

From Glacier National Park, we flew to Seattle to meet a few friends. We had only a day to explore and it was a tough choice between Rainier and Olympic National Parks. We ultimately chose Olympic for its diverse ecosystems and geological features.

Olympic National Park

Olympic National Park is a geological wonderland that showcases the complex tectonic history of the Pacific Northwest. The park sits on the Olympic Peninsula, which is composed of accreted oceanic terranes that were scraped off the oceanic plate and added to the North American continent over millions of years.

The lush temperate rainforest of Olympic National Park

What struck me most was the incredible diversity of rock types and ages within a relatively small area. From Paleozoic to Mesozoic marine sedimentary rocks to Miocene basalts, the park tells a story of oceanic crust formation, subduction, and accretion.

The Temperate Rainforest

The Hoh Rainforest was absolutely magical. The combination of high precipitation, mild temperatures, and the underlying geology creates perfect conditions for this unique ecosystem. The thick soil development over the weathered marine sedimentary rocks provides excellent drainage and nutrient availability for the massive Sitka spruces and western hemlocks.

Coastal Geology

Dramatic sea stacks along the Olympic Peninsula coast

The coastal section of the park showcases spectacular examples of marine erosion and deposition. The sea stacks and arches are carved from resistant Miocene basalts and marine sedimentary rocks. The wave-cut platforms expose beautiful cross-sections of the geological formations, providing excellent opportunities to study the structural relationships between different rock units.

Great Smoky Mountains

The ancient Appalachian Mountains shrouded in mist

Our final stop was the Great Smoky Mountains, which provided a fascinating contrast to the young, tectonically active geology of the western parks. Here, we're looking at some of the oldest rocks in North America - Precambrian basement rocks that have been through multiple orogenic events.

The Smokies are part of the Appalachian Mountain system, formed during the assembly of the supercontinent Pangaea. The complex thrust faulting and folding visible throughout the park tells the story of continental collision and the closure of the ancient Iapetus Ocean.

Geological Observations

  • Excellent exposures of Precambrian gneisses and schists in the Smoky Mountains
  • Beautiful examples of Paleozoic thrust faulting and structural geology
  • Diverse metamorphic mineral assemblages indicating varying P-T conditions
  • Evidence of multiple deformation events in the Appalachian orogeny
  • Contrast between ancient, stable continental crust and active margin tectonics

Research Connections

This trip provided excellent field experience that directly relates to my research on metamorphic processes. Seeing the regional metamorphic rocks of the Appalachians helped me better understand the large-scale processes that I study in the laboratory. The contrast between contact and regional metamorphism was particularly enlightening.

The diverse geological settings also highlighted the importance of tectonic context in understanding metamorphic processes. From the accretionary complex of Olympic to the continental collision zone of the Appalachians, each setting produces distinct metamorphic signatures that tell unique stories about Earth's dynamic history.

Final Thoughts

This summer of national parks was not just a vacation, but an incredible field geology experience that has enriched my understanding of North American geology. From the young, active margins of the west to the ancient, stable craton of the east, each park offered unique insights into the processes that have shaped our continent.

As a geologist, there's nothing quite like seeing these processes written in stone across such spectacular landscapes. These experiences continue to inspire my research and remind me why I fell in love with geology in the first place.
